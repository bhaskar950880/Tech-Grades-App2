PACE Satellite

Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, ocean Ecosystem (PACE) is a 2020s NASA Earth-observing satellite mission observing global ocean color, biogeochemistry, and ecology, as well as the carbon cycle, aerosols and clouds. PACE is intended to be used to identify the extent and duration of phytoplankton blooms and improve understanding of air quality.

After being proposed for cancellation under President Trump's FY 2018 budget, the program was restored by Congress in 2018. On 4 February 2020, NASA announced the selection of SpaceX to launch PACE on a Falcon 9, at a total cost to NASA of US$80.4 million. PACE successfully launched on 8 February 2024 at 06:33 UTC.

Oceans

Oceans Color and Water Quality

Advanced Ocean Color Detection: Ocean color is a critical parameter for understanding water quality, marine habitats, and biological productivity. PACE offers unprecedented hyperspectral measurements that span the ultraviolet (UV) to near-infrared (NIR) spectrum. These sensors detect subtle changes in the color of the ocean surface, which can indicate biological or chemical processes happening within.

Reports Generated

Harmful Algal Bloom (HAB) Detection: PACE identifies and tracks the development of HABs, which can cause fish die-offs, contaminate seafood, and negatively impact human health. These blooms are often detected through changes in water color caused by specific phytoplankton species.

Sediment Transport and Pollution Tracking: The satellite monitors sediment plumes from rivers and coastal runoff, allowing for real-time assessments of water quality, especially after storms or floods.

Coral Reef and Seagrass Habitat Mapping: By observing shallow coastal waters, PACE provides data on coral reef health and the state of underwater vegetation, such as seagrass, which are key to coastal ecosystems.

Significance

Timely detection of water quality issues and harmful blooms is essential for protecting marine ecosystems, public health, and coastal economies reliant on fisheries and tourism.

Climate Change and Ocean-Atmosphere Interaction

Carbon Cycle and Climate Regulation: The ocean is a major carbon sink, absorbing roughly 25% of human-produced CO₂. Phytoplankton in the ocean absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis, and when they die, they can transport carbon to the deep ocean, sequestering it for centuries. PACE provides the detailed information necessary to understand these processes on a global scale.

Report Generated

Carbon Sequestration Data: Using ocean color data, PACE can estimate how much carbon is being absorbed by the ocean, and how much is being sequestered through the "biological pump" — the process by which organic material sinks to the deep ocean.

Global Carbon Flux Reports: These are detailed analyses of how oceanic carbon cycling interacts with atmospheric CO₂ levels, helping refine climate models.

Ocean Heat and Carbon Absorption Maps: PACE helps scientists track how much heat and carbon the ocean is absorbing, a critical component of understanding global climate dynamics and predicting future climate changes.

Significance

As the Earth warms, changes in the ocean’s ability to absorb CO₂ and heat will have profound implications for climate regulation. PACE’s data will help predict how these processes might evolve, guiding global climate policy.

Planktons

Plankton are tiny organisms that play an essential role in ocean ecosystems and the global carbon cycle. These microscopic organisms, which include both phytoplankton (plant-like organisms) and zooplankton (tiny animal-like organisms), form the foundation of the marine food web. Their health and abundance are critical indicators of oceanic and atmospheric changes.

Types of Planktons

  • Phytoplanktons

    • Phytoplankton are microscopic, photosynthetic organisms (much like plants) that live in the upper layers of the ocean.

    They produce around 50% of the Earth's oxygen, making them crucial for maintaining atmospheric oxygen levels.

    Phytoplankton absorb carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, thus playing a significant role in the carbon cycle and mitigating the impact of climate change.

  • Zooplankton

    • Zooplankton are tiny animals that feed on phytoplankton and other small particles in the ocean.

    They are crucial in transferring energy up the food chain, being a primary food source for larger marine animals, including fish and whales.

How PACE Satellite Provides Information on Plankton

The PACE mission is equipped with an advanced Ocean Color Instrument (OCI), designed to monitor ocean color across a wide spectrum of wavelengths. This allows scientists to study both the concentration and composition of plankton. Here’s how PACE contributes to understanding plankton:

  • Phytoplankton Biomass Monitoring

    • By analyzing the color of the ocean, PACE can determine the concentration of chlorophyll a pigment found in phytoplankton. Chlorophyll concentrations serve as an indicator of phytoplankton biomass and the overall health of marine ecosystems.

  • Phytoplankton Diversity and Composition

    • PACE’s hyperspectral imaging capability goes beyond basic chlorophyll detection. It can differentiate between different types of phytoplankton based on their spectral signatures.

  • Tracking Climate Change Impacts

    • PACE’s long-term monitoring helps track how climate change is affecting phytoplankton distribution and abundance. For example, warming waters can cause shifts in the types of phytoplankton that thrive in certain areas, impacting the food web.

  • Ocean Carbon Cycle

    • Phytoplankton play a significant role in the biological carbon pump, a process where carbon is absorbed from the atmosphere and stored in the ocean. PACE helps quantify how much carbon is being taken up by phytoplankton during photosynthesis.

  • Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs) Detection

    • HAB reports provided by PACE allow local authorities and governments to track these blooms and take preventative or protective actions to safeguard coastal ecosystems and human populations.

Clouds

The PACE satellite is equipped with sophisticated instruments designed to provide high-quality, continuous data on cloud properties, aerosol interactions, and radiative processes that are vital for understanding climate dynamics.

Key Contributions of PACE to Cloud Studies

Cloud Optical and Microphysical Properties:

  • Cloud optical thickness (COT): Measures the degree to which clouds block sunlight.
  • Cloud particle size and composition: Helps determine if clouds are made of water droplets, ice crystals, or mixed-phase particles.
  • Cloud reflectance: Affects how clouds reflect sunlight, which plays a role in Earth’s radiation budget.

Aerosol-Cloud Interactions

  • Aerosols (tiny particles in the atmosphere) influence cloud formation and behavior. They can act as cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) or ice nucleating particles, helping to form cloud droplets or ice crystals.
  • The interactions between aerosols and clouds are complex, impacting cloud lifetime, precipitation patterns, and cloud albedo (brightness).

Cloud-Radiative Forcing

  • High, thin clouds may trap outgoing infrared radiation (warming effect).
  • Low, thick clouds tend to reflect more incoming sunlight (cooling effect).

Improving Weather and Climate Models

  • By providing better data on cloud-aerosol interactions, cloud formation, and their global distribution, PACE helps to improve weather prediction and climate models.
  • PACE’s data are critical for accurately modeling cloud feedback mechanisms, which are among the most uncertain aspects of climate science. These feedbacks are crucial to predicting future global warming scenarios.

Instruments on PACE and Their Role in Cloud Monitoring

Ocean Color Instrument (OCI):

  • The OCI is PACE's primary sensor, designed to monitor ocean color, but it also measures cloud properties across visible, ultraviolet, and near-infrared wavelengths.
  • It can differentiate between different types of clouds, assess their optical thickness, and provide data on cloud cover across the globe.

Multi-Angle Polarimeter (MAP)

  • The MAP provides polarization data, which is critical for distinguishing between aerosols, cloud droplets, and ice particles.
  • By capturing light at multiple angles, this instrument can provide detailed information on the microphysical properties of clouds, such as droplet size, ice particle shape, and cloud phase (liquid or ice).

Hyper-Angular Rainbow Polarimeter (HARP2)

  • This instrument is designed to capture cloud particle size distributions and determine whether clouds contain water droplets, ice crystals, or a mixture.
  • HARP2 offers multi-angle views of clouds, which improve the accuracy of cloud measurements and provide deeper insights into cloud-aerosol interactions.
